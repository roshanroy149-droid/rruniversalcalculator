How is the loan payment formula calculated?

This uses the standard fixed-rate amortization formula: it converts your annual rate to a monthly rate, then solves for a level payment that pays off the balance exactly across the full term.

Does this include taxes, insurance, or fees?

No — this is principal and interest only. Mortgages and some auto loans often bundle in taxes, insurance, or other fees, which would raise your actual monthly payment above this number.

Why does total interest matter?

Two loans with the same monthly payment can cost very differently over time depending on the rate and term — comparing total interest, not just the monthly number, shows the real cost.

What's the difference between flat rate and reducing balance interest?

This calculator uses reducing balance (amortizing) interest, the standard for most personal loans, auto loans, and mortgages — interest is charged only on the remaining principal each month, so the interest portion shrinks over time even though the total payment stays level. Flat rate interest (less common, sometimes used for short-term loans) charges interest on the original amount for the entire term, which works out more expensive for the same stated rate.

Why does the total interest change so much when I extend the term?

A longer term lowers the monthly payment but increases total interest paid, since you're borrowing the lender's money for more months. Try comparing a 36-month and 60-month term on the same loan amount above — the monthly payment drops, but total interest paid rises noticeably.

Worked example: a $20,000 loan at 6.5% annual interest over 60 months has a monthly payment of about $391 and total interest of roughly $3,460 over the life of the loan — try these exact numbers above to see the calculator match this.
