How is a traditional pension calculated?

Most defined benefit pensions use a straightforward formula: annual pension = final average salary × accrual rate × years of service. The accrual rate is a percentage set by the specific plan — commonly somewhere around 1-2% per year of service — and "final average salary" is typically an average of your highest-earning consecutive years (often the final 3 or 5), not simply your very last paycheck. Multiply the three inputs together and you get your estimated annual benefit at retirement.

What is an accrual rate and how do I find mine?

The accrual rate is the percentage of your final average salary you earn as annual pension for every year you worked under the plan — it's set entirely by your specific pension plan's rules, not a universal figure, and can vary meaningfully between employers, job types, and even tiers within the same plan (some plans reduce the accrual rate for years worked past a certain threshold). Check your plan's summary plan description, your employer's benefits portal, or ask your HR or pension administrator directly for the exact rate that applies to you.

Pension vs. 401(k) — what's the difference?

A pension (a "defined benefit" plan) promises a specific payout formula regardless of how any underlying investments perform — the employer bears the investment risk and is on the hook to fund the promised benefit. A 401(k) (a "defined contribution" plan) instead depends entirely on how much is contributed and how those contributions perform in the market, with the employee bearing all of the investment risk and reward. See the 401(k) Calculator to model that side of retirement savings separately.

Are pensions still common?

Traditional private-sector pensions have become far less common than they were a few decades ago, as most private employers shifted toward 401(k)-style defined contribution plans, which shift both cost and investment risk off the employer's balance sheet. Pensions remain much more common in the public sector, however — many government, military, and unionized jobs still offer a defined benefit pension, often alongside a supplemental defined contribution plan.

Worked example: 25 years of service, a $70,000 final average salary, and a 1.5% accrual rate: annual pension = 70,000 × 0.015 × 25 = $26,250 per year, or $2,187.50 per month — equal to 37.5% of the final average salary. See the Retirement Calculator to combine this pension income with other savings and estimate your overall retirement readiness.
