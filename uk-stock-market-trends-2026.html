The FTSE's "boring" stocks are having a moment. So is Rolls-Royce.

Banks, miners, and defence contractors have turned the FTSE 100's old-economy reputation into an actual advantage in 2026 — up 19.3% while AI names elsewhere whipsaw. And nothing captures the market's mood quite like Rolls-Royce's ongoing turnaround story.

By TallyBench Team · Updated July 22, 2026 · 7 min read

The FTSE 100's story in 2026 is, in a way, about what it isn't. While the Nasdaq and S&P 500 have been buffeted by dramatic swings in AI-linked names, London's flagship index has climbed roughly 19.3% year-to-date through June, precisely because its heaviest sectors — banks, mining, defence, and healthcare — sit largely outside that AI infrastructure cycle. That's turned a long-running criticism of the UK market (too much "old economy," not enough growth-tech) into a genuine stabilizing advantage this year. And no single stock captures the market's current mood better than Rolls-Royce, widely described as the defining UK equity story of the past three years.

The short version: FTSE 100 up ~19.3% year-to-date through June 2026, building on a 2025 in which it gained 21.51%, broke above 10,000 points for the first time in its history, and even outpaced the S&P 500 in local-currency terms. It closed at a record 10,686 on February 19, 2026. The rally's engine has been "old economy" sectors — banks, miners, defence contractors, healthcare — acting as a stabilizer against the sharp AI-linked swings hitting US tech indexes. Rolls-Royce remains under heavy investor attention as the index's most-discussed individual turnaround story.

Why "boring" has been an advantage in 2026

The FTSE 100's sector composition has historically been treated as a weakness relative to growth-tech-heavy US indexes — but 2026 has flipped that framing. Banks, mining companies, defence contractors, and healthcare businesses, which make up a large share of the index, are largely disconnected from the AI infrastructure spending cycle that's driven big swings in Nasdaq and S&P 500 names. That's given the FTSE a genuinely stabilizing character this year: it hasn't captured the most explosive single-day AI-driven gains, but it also hasn't been exposed to the sharp reversals that theme has produced elsewhere, like the mid-July semiconductor rotation seen in US markets.

Record highs, in context

The FTSE 100 closed at a record 10,686 points on February 19, 2026, its second consecutive record close at the time, and has traded not far below that level since — around 10,508 as of late June. That builds directly on a standout 2025, when the index gained 21.51%, broke the psychologically significant 10,000-point barrier for the first time in its history, and even outpaced the US S&P 500 in local-currency terms. Analysts see room for further gains in 2026, though they're explicit that uncertainty around global markets, currency fluctuations, UK inflation, and interest rates remain real variables — and that renewed AI-driven US outperformance could itself become a headwind, pulling capital back toward American growth stocks.

Rolls-Royce: the stock everyone's still watching

If one name defines UK equity conversation right now, it's Rolls-Royce — described as the defining UK equity story of the past three years, and one that remains under close investor watch throughout 2026 rather than being treated as old news. Its arc from crisis to recovery has made it something of a bellwether for UK "turnaround" investing generally, the kind of stock that comes up in conversation well beyond specialist aerospace or defence circles. Alongside it, BAE Systems — Europe's largest defence contractor by revenue — reported record 2025 sales of £30.7 billion and continues winning major contracts globally, giving the UK's defence sector a second high-profile name drawing sustained attention in 2026.

Other names drawing attention

Beyond Rolls-Royce and BAE, a handful of other UK names have featured heavily in 2026 investor conversation: RELX, Experian, and Melrose Industries have been tipped by analysts to outperform, with RELX showing the highest growth expectations among the three at 71%. HSBC remains one of the most widely held UK income stocks, with a dividend yield around 6-7% that continues to appeal to investors prioritizing steady income over growth. These names round out a market where both growth expectations and defensive income appeal are in genuine demand simultaneously — a reasonably unusual combination.

What this means if you're watching UK markets

  • Sector composition, not just headline growth rate, explains why the FTSE has behaved differently from AI-heavy indexes this year — worth understanding before assuming one index's strategy applies to another.
  • A stabilizing 2026 doesn't mean risk-free — currency moves, UK rates, and a potential swing back toward US AI outperformance are all named risks to the FTSE's current run.
  • Turnaround stories like Rolls-Royce draw sustained attention well past their initial recovery phase — being "the story of the last three years" doesn't mean the story is necessarily finished.

Why has the FTSE 100 done well while AI stocks in the US have been volatile?

The FTSE 100's sector mix — banks, mining companies, defence contractors, and healthcare businesses — is heavily weighted toward what's often called "old economy" stocks, which are largely disconnected from the AI infrastructure spending cycle that's been driving big swings in Nasdaq and S&P 500 tech names. That's given the index a stabilizing character in 2026: less explosive upside on any single AI headline, but also less exposure to the sharp reversals that theme has produced elsewhere.

Could the FTSE 100's outperformance versus AI-heavy US indexes reverse?

It's a genuine risk worth naming directly: if AI-driven US stocks resume strongly outperforming, that could act as a headwind for UK investors who've benefited from the FTSE's more defensive positioning, since capital could rotate back toward the US growth theme. UK-specific factors — inflation, interest rates, and sterling's exchange rate — are also cited as real variables that could influence the index's path from here, independent of what happens in AI-linked markets elsewhere.

Is Rolls-Royce's stock still considered a turnaround story in 2026, or has it already turned around?

It's been described as the defining UK equity story of the past three years specifically because it moved from crisis to recovery within recent memory, and it remains under close investor watch throughout 2026 — but this article is not a recommendation to buy, hold, or sell the stock. Whether a turnaround is "complete" or still has further to run is a judgment call for individual investors and their own research, not something a general market overview can settle.
