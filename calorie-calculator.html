What formula does this use?

BMR is calculated with the Mifflin-St Jeor equation — 10×weight(kg) + 6.25×height(cm) − 5×age + 5 for men, and the same minus 161 instead of plus 5 for women. Published in 1990 and re-validated repeatedly since, it's generally considered more accurate for the modern population than the older 1919 Harris-Benedict formula, which tends to overestimate BMR by 5–15% for most people today. TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) multiplies BMR by an activity factor to estimate total calories burned per day, including exercise and general movement.

How does Harris-Benedict compare, and should I care?

Harris-Benedict (revised 1984 version: 88.36 + 13.4×kg + 4.8×cm − 5.7×age for men) is still widely quoted and used in some clinical settings, but independent validation studies consistently find Mifflin-St Jeor closer to measured resting metabolic rate for the general population, which is why this calculator defaults to it. The two formulas typically land within 3–5% of each other for an average-build adult — the gap widens for people at the extremes of body composition, which is exactly the case Katch-McArdle below is built for.

What about the Katch-McArdle formula?

Katch-McArdle calculates BMR from lean body mass instead of total weight: 370 + 21.6 × lean mass (kg). Because fat tissue burns far fewer calories at rest than muscle, this formula is more accurate for people who are notably lean or muscular — bodybuilders, athletes — where Mifflin-St Jeor's weight-only approach can misjudge BMR. Its accuracy depends entirely on an accurate body-fat percentage input, which most people don't have precisely measured (a DEXA scan or hydrostatic weighing gives the most reliable figure; bioelectrical scales are a rough estimate at best) — that's why this calculator uses Mifflin-St Jeor as the general-purpose default.

How are the weight loss and gain targets calculated?

Weight loss target subtracts 500 calories from TDEE, and weight gain target adds 500 — a 500-calorie daily deficit or surplus roughly corresponds to about 0.45 kg (1 lb) of change per week, since one pound of body fat represents approximately 3,500 kcal. This is a moderate, sustainable pace recommended by most dietitians; a more aggressive 750–1,000 calorie deficit accelerates loss but is harder to sustain and risks more muscle loss alongside fat.

What do the macro numbers mean?

Protein and carbohydrates provide 4 kcal per gram, fat provides 9 kcal per gram — the macro breakdown above splits your maintenance calories into grams of each using whichever style you pick. "Balanced" (30/40/30) suits general health goals; "Higher protein" (40/30/30) supports muscle retention during a calorie deficit or muscle gain during a surplus, since protein needs don't scale down just because calories do; "Lower carb" (35/20/45) suits people managing blood sugar or following a lower-carbohydrate eating pattern. None of these is more "correct" than another — the right split depends on your goal, medical situation, and what you can sustain.

Is this accurate for everyone?

These are population-average formulas — actual metabolism varies with muscle mass, genetics, thyroid function, and other health conditions, and studies show even the best equations can be off by 10% or more for a given individual. Treat the numbers as a well-informed starting point, not a precise measurement, and adjust based on how your actual weight responds over 2–3 weeks of consistent tracking — that real-world feedback beats any formula.

Should I eat exactly my TDEE every day?

TDEE is a maintenance estimate, not a daily prescription — actual intake naturally varies day to day with social meals, travel, and appetite, and what matters for weight management is the average over a week or two, not hitting an exact number every single day. Weekly averaging also smooths out water-weight fluctuations that make day-to-day scale readings noisy.

Does this differ between the US, Europe, and India?

The physiology and formulas are universal — a calorie is a calorie regardless of country. What differs is units and labeling conventions: US nutrition labels use Calories (kcal) and often report macros in grams per serving with different serving-size conventions than the EU's per-100g standard; India's ICMR-NIN also publishes its own reference energy intake tables, which run somewhat lower than US RDA figures for a given body size, reflecting differences in the reference population studied. If you're cross-referencing a food label from a different country, the calorie and gram figures are directly comparable — just check whether you're looking at "per serving" or "per 100g."

Worked example: a 30-year-old male, 75 kg, 178 cm, moderately active: BMR = 10×75 + 6.25×178 − 5×30 + 5 = 750+1,112.5−150+5 = 1,717.5 kcal. TDEE = 1,717.5 × 1.55 ≈ 2,662 kcal/day for maintenance. On a balanced macro split, that's roughly 200g protein, 266g carbs, and 89g fat per day (30/40/30 by calories, converted at 4/4/9 kcal per gram).
