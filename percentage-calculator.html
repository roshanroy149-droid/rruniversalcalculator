How do you calculate X percent of Y?

Multiply Y by X, then divide by 100. For example, 20% of 150 is (150 × 20) ÷ 100 = 30.

How do you find what percent A is of B?

Divide A by B, then multiply by 100. For example, 30 is what percent of 120? (30 ÷ 120) × 100 = 25%.

Where does this come up in real life?

Discounts, tax rates, grade calculations, tip splitting, and comparing changes over time all reduce to one of these two percent problems.

How do you calculate a percentage increase or decrease?

Subtract the old value from the new value, divide by the old value, then multiply by 100. A price moving from $80 to $100 is a (100−80)/80×100 = 25% increase. A drop from $100 to $80 is (80−100)/100×100 = −20% — note the percentages aren't symmetric, because the base changes.

What's the difference between a percent and a percentage point?

If an interest rate moves from 5% to 7%, that's a 2 percentage point increase, but a 40% relative increase in the rate itself (2÷5×100). News headlines often blur this distinction — always check which one is being reported.

Worked example: a shirt is discounted from $45 to $36. Percent decrease = (45−36)/45×100 = 20%. Using the calculator above, entering 20 as X and 45 as Y in the top field gives the discount amount directly (9), matching this by-hand result.
