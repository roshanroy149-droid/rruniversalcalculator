// US MARKET WEEK

Iran tensions, oil, and Alphabet's capex raise sent Wall Street lower this week.

The Nasdaq, S&P 500, and Dow all closed the week of July 20 in the red as an escalating Iran conflict pushed oil prices higher and Alphabet's bigger-than-expected 2026 spending forecast overshadowed a strong earnings beat. Here's what actually moved the market, day by day.

By TallyBench Team · Updated July 24, 2026 · 6 min read

Wall Street closed lower for the week of July 20, 2026, with all three major benchmarks in the red: the Nasdaq Composite fell 2.9%, the S&P 500 fell 1.6%, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 0.9%. The week didn't move in a straight line — Monday and Wednesday were only modestly down, before a sharper Thursday selloff dragged the week's total lower — and the two forces behind almost every session were the same: an escalating conflict involving Iran that pushed oil prices up, and second-quarter earnings season colliding with fresh worry about how much Big Tech is willing to spend on AI infrastructure.

The short version: Nasdaq -2.9%, S&P 500 -1.6%, Dow -0.9% for the week. Thursday alone: Dow -506.93 points (-1%) to 51,711.65, Nasdaq -553.21 points (-2.2%) to 25,137.69, S&P 500 -90.66 points (-1.2%) to 7,408.30. Drivers: President Trump declaring the Strait of Hormuz ceasefire over after US strikes on Iranian coastal targets, pushing oil prices higher; and Alphabet raising its 2026 capex forecast to $195-205 billion even after beating earnings estimates, reviving concern about AI-spending payback.

Iran tensions and oil set the tone all week

The week's dominant story wasn't a company at all — it was the Strait of Hormuz. President Trump declared the region's fragile ceasefire over following US military strikes on targets along the Iranian coastline, in response to what appeared to be Iranian attacks on vessels transiting the strait. Iran reportedly asked Yemen's Houthi movement to be ready to close the Red Sea oil export route if the US struck Iranian power plants and bridges, a threat that, combined with the Strait of Hormuz, covers two of the world's most important oil shipping chokepoints. Oil prices rose on the news, and equities pulled back in response, a fairly standard market reaction to geopolitical risk around energy supply. One modest counterweight: Iran's release of a detained US citizen this week was read by some investors as a small de-escalation signal, though it wasn't enough to reverse the week's losses.

Alphabet's capex raise overshadowed a strong earnings beat

Second-quarter earnings season is going well by the numbers: of the 95 S&P 500 companies that had reported by Thursday, 88% beat estimates, by a median margin of 7%, while the 12% that missed did so by an average of just 2%. Alphabet's own results fit that pattern — $9.11 in earnings per share against a $2.88 estimate, and revenue of $103.62 billion, both comfortably ahead of expectations. But Alphabet's stock still weighed on the broader market after the company raised its full-year 2026 capital expenditure forecast to $195-205 billion, up from $180-190 billion, as part of continued heavy AI infrastructure investment. Investors read the bigger number as a sign that AI spending among the largest tech companies keeps climbing with no clear ceiling in sight, reviving a worry that's shadowed the sector for much of 2026: that the payoff on all this spending is further away than the spending itself.

What this means if you're investing through it

  • A strong earnings season doesn't guarantee a strong market week — an 88% beat rate across reporting companies still coincided with a down week, because geopolitical risk and forward spending guidance moved sentiment more than the quarter that just ended.
  • Oil-price-driven selloffs tend to be event-specific — worth distinguishing from a broader shift in growth expectations, since they typically ease if the underlying geopolitical risk does.
  • Rising AI capex is now a two-sided story — the same spending increase that reassures investors about future AI capability also raises near-term questions about return on that spending, and markets are increasingly pricing both sides at once.

Why did the US stock market fall this week?

Two things dominated the week of July 20, 2026: escalating Iran tensions after President Trump declared the Strait of Hormuz ceasefire over following US strikes on Iranian coastal targets, which pushed oil prices higher and dented risk appetite, and renewed anxiety over AI infrastructure spending after Alphabet raised its 2026 capital expenditure forecast. For the week, the Nasdaq fell 2.9%, the S&P 500 fell 1.6%, and the Dow fell 0.9%.

If earnings were strong, why did the market still fall?

Of the 95 S&P 500 companies that had reported by Thursday, 88% beat earnings estimates by a median of 7%, including Alphabet, which posted $9.11 in earnings per share against a $2.88 estimate. But Alphabet's stock fell anyway after the company raised its 2026 capex guidance to $195-205 billion from $180-190 billion — investors reacted to the bigger spending number, not the earnings beat, intensifying a broader worry that AI infrastructure spending is outrunning near-term returns.

What's happening with Iran, and why does it move stock prices?

President Trump declared the fragile ceasefire in the Strait of Hormuz over after US strikes on targets along the Iranian coastline, and Iran reportedly asked Yemen's Houthi movement to be ready to close the Red Sea oil export route in response. Both waterways carry a large share of the world's seaborne oil, so any real threat to them pushes oil prices up and, historically, weighs on equities — though Iran's release of a US citizen this week was read by some investors as a small de-escalation signal.
